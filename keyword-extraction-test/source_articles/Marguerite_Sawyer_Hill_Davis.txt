'''Marguerite Sawyer Hill Davis''' (died 1948) was one of the wealthiest women in the United States in the mid 1900s. Her husband, James Norman Hill, was the son of [[James J. Hill]], a railroad tycoon.{{r|Fulton}} She was an art patron, socialite, and heiress to a railroad fortune.<ref name=SHSM>{{cite web | url=http://shs.umsystem.edu/kansascity/manuscripts/k0031.pdf | title=Blevins Davis (1903-1971) Papers | publisher= The State Historical Society of Missouri Research Center-Kansas City | accessdate=February 17, 2015 }}</ref>

==Early life==
Marguerite, also known as Margaret, was the daughter of Arthur Wilkinson Sawyer, of Massachusetts Institute of Technology class of 1872, and Caroline A. Lodge Sawyer.<ref name=Yale>{{cite web | url=http://mssa.library.yale.edu/obituary_record/1925_1952/1932-33.pdf | title=Obituary Record of Graduates, Yale University, 1932-1933 | publisher=Yale University | accessdate=February 17, 2015 }}</ref> She had a sister and two brothers.<ref name="Farley">{{cite book|author=Arthur C. Farley|title=Technology Review|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=wkULAAAAYAAJ&pg=PA705|year=1918|publisher=Association of Alumni and Alumnae of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology|page=705|chapter=Arthur Wilkinson Sawyer}}</ref> Both of her parents came from wealth background, her mother a member of Boston's [[Lodge family]]. Marguerite attended Miss Claggett's School as a young girl and then traveled throughout Europe with a governess for one year.{{r|Splendid Suitors}} Her father was the son of Joseph Sawyer, part-owner of E. R. Mudge, Sawyer & Company, one of the country's most important dry goods commission houses of the time. Arthur was in business with his father before becoming the Boston Young Men's Christian Association president, a position he held for two years. He retired to the Pacific Coast and Hawaiian Islands.{{r|Farley}}

==Adulthood==
Marguerite Sawyer was married to Dr. Clarence Fahnestock on June 5, 1906 in [[Marion, Massachusetts]]. His parents were M.A. McKinley and banker Harris C. McKinley Fahnestock.<ref>{{citation | title=June 5, 1906 marriage of Clarence Fahnestock and Marguerite Sawyer | work=Marion Births, Marriages and Deaths (1852-1910), Town and City Clerks of Massachusetts. Massachusetts Vital and Town Records }}</ref>{{r|Fahnestock obit}} Clarence was a physician, and big game hunter.{{r|Fahnestock obit}} The Fahnestocks  divorced,<ref name=Syracuse>{{cite news |title=Mrs. James N. Hill, Rail Man's Widow, Weds for 3rd Time | date=October 4, 1933|newspaper=Syracuse Herald | location=Syracuse, New York | page=14 }}</ref> and Marguerite went to Europe.{{r|Splendid Suitors}} Clarence gave up his practice in New York to join the army during [[World War I]] and became the chief surgeon for the 301st Infantry and service in France. He died there of pneumonia in 1918.<ref name="Fahnestock obit">{{cite news | title=Major Fahnestock Dead of Pneumonia: West to France as Line Officer and Became Chief Surgeon of the 301st Infantry |newspaper=New York Times| date=October 9, 1918 }}</ref>

Marguerite Sawyer Fahnestock married James Norman Hill (born 1870), the son of railroad tycoon James J. Hill.{{r|Fulton}}{{r|MNHS}} Hill was the vice-president of [[Great Northern Railway (U.S.)|Great Northern Railway]] in 1905, trustee of Great Northern Iron Ore Properties, and president of United Securities Company of St. Paul.<ref name="Ingham">{{cite book|author=John N. Ingham|title=Biographical Dictionary of American Business Leaders|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=Suu9yUKdA8IC&pg=PA582|year=1983|publisher=Greenwood Publishing Group|isbn=978-0-313-23908-3|page=582}}</ref> Marguerite and James were married in London on August 23, 1912{{r|MNHS}} at a Registry Office and then in Savory at the [[Savoy Chapel|Chapel Royal]].<ref name=NYT>{{cite news | url=http://query.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=9802E4DA1630E233A25757C2A96E9C946396D6CF |title=James N. Hill Takes Bride: Son of James J. Hill Marries Mrs. Fahnestock In London | newspaper=New York Times | date=August 24, 1912 | accessdate=February 17, 2015 }}</ref> The wedding was a quiet ceremony that was not attended by any of Hill's family members.{{r|MNHS}} Margaret wore a large picture hat and a traveling costume. The wife of Admiral [[William T. Swinburne|Swinburne]] was one of the half dozen people who attended the wedding. They had a luncheon celebration with the wedding party and Mr. and Mrs. [[De Lancey Nicoll]] at [[Claridge's]]. The Hills honeymooned on an automobile trip down the south coast of England from [[Margate]].{{r|NYT}} Her sister Pauline married James' brother, Walter.{{r|Splendid Suitors}}

Marguerite was never received at the [[James J. Hill House]] in [[Saint Paul, Minnesota]], "apparently because she was a divorcee."{{r|MNHS}}  James N. Hill became quite wealthy due to his investment in Texas Company, later [[Texaco]].<ref name=MNHS>{{cite web | url=http://sites.mnhs.org/historic-sites/james-j-hill-house/james-norman-hill | title=James Norman Hill | publisher=Minnesota Historical Society | accessdate=February 17, 2015 }}</ref> The Hills owned Big Tree Farm on Wheatley Road in [[Brookville, New York]] on [[Long Island]].{{r|Princeton}}{{r|Mateyunas}} [[Walker & Gillette]] designed the building and the landscaping was designed by the [[Olmsted Brothers]].{{r|Mateyunas}} On their 230-acre estate, they bred Holstein and Ayrshire cattle, grew 769 species of trees, and had three large duck ponds. The Hill's interests included arborculture, gardening, horses, tennis, shooting and golf. The Hill's home at [[Wheatley Hills, New York]], considered one of the east coast's show places{{r|Fulton}}, was the site of lavish events.{{r|Splendid Suitors}} They were on the [[Social Register]].<ref>{{cite book|title=Social Register: Contains the Summer Address where it Differs from the Winter Address of the Residents of New York, Washington, Philadelphia, Chicago, Boston, St. Louis, Pittsburgh, Cleveland, etc.|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=9RQJAQAAIAAJ&pg=PA130|year=1919|publisher=Social Register Association.|page=130}}</ref>

The Hills did not have any children.{{r|MNHS}} He died in 1932 and Margaret was said to have been left with an estate estimated at $80 million,{{r|Fulton}} but she was only left $2 million of the Hill fortune.{{r|Splendid Suitors}} Her husband was buried at the James Norman Hill mausoleum in [[Woodlawn Cemetery (Bronx)|Woodland Cemetery]] in [[The Bronx|Bronx, New York]].<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.nps.gov/nhl/news/LC/fall2010/Woodlawn.pdf | title=Woodlawn Cemetery, National Historic Landmark Nomination | publisher=US National Park Service |page=30 |date=March 10, 2010| accessdate=February 17, 2015 }}</ref> Marguerite grew the money she was left from her husband from $2 to $9 million, she said, by the efforts of her financial adviser and common sense.{{r|Splendid Suitors}}

Marguerite married Herbert Neal, a descendant of [[John Adams]], on October 3, 1933 in Elkton, Maryland. He worked for Standard Oil Company of New York for 20 years.{{r|Syracuse}}{{r|American Weekly}} They spent their honeymoon in South America, France, and Spain. Shortly after, she returned from a trip to Honolulu and found that Herbert Neal was staying with another woman in a New Orleans Hotel. They were divorced in 1937.{{r|Splendid Suitors}} After the divorce, she resumed the name Marguerite Hill. She then entertained at Big Tree Farm in New York, Palm Beach, and in Washington, D.C. and dated a number of society men.<ref name="American Weekly">{{cite journal | title=Marge Hill's Surprise Choice | author=William Engle | journal=The American Weekly | date=January 18, 1947 }}</ref> She was good friends and vacationed with David S. Cowles, Baron George Wrangall, and Jean Saint Cyr. Wrangall had given a toast to her, "the most glorious woman in the world."{{r|Splendid Suitors}} On Long Island, she favored clam bakes and sometimes had impromptu parties that lasted a week.{{r|Splendid Suitors}} She had a large collection of  jewelry  featuring diamonds, emeralds, rubys, sapphires, and other gems; the collection was stolen from her jewel safe at the Big Tree Farm in October, 1942. The collection including heirloom pieces, and was worth at least $350,000 ({{inflation|US|350,000|1942|fmt=eq}}), but she soon began to rebuild a collection.<ref name="Splendid Suitors">{{cite news | title=Splendid Marge Hill's Many Suitors | newspaper=The American Weekly, The Milwaukee Sentinel | location=Milwaukee, Wisconsin |page=32 |date=June 20, 1948 }}</ref>

In 1946,{{r|Fulton}} Marguerite married [[Blevins Davis]] at the Mayflower Hotel in Washington, D.C.{{r|American Weekly}} The ceremony was attended by [[Bess Truman]].{{r|Fulton}} The [[Harry S. Truman]] family had been neighbors and life-long friends of her husband.<ref name="Princeton">{{cite book|title=Princeton Alumni Weekly|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=dRlbAAAAYAAJ&pg=PP18|year=1971|publisher=princeton alumni weekly|page=18|id=PRNC:32101081977058}}</ref> After the wedding, the couple took the ship ''America'' to Europe for their honeymoon.{{r|American Weekly}}

Davis died while traveling from California back to her home in New York.{{r|Splendid Suitors}} She died at St. Luke's Hospital in [[Kansas City, Missouri]] on March 18, 1948 following a heart attack.<ref name=Fulton>{{cite news | url=http://fultonhistory.com/newspaper%2010/Kingston%20NY%20Daily%20Freeman/Kingston%20NY%20Daily%20Freeman%201948%20Grayscale/Kingston%20NY%20Daily%20Freeman%201948%20Grayscale%20-%200975.pdf|title=Mrs. Davis Dies|newspaper=The Kingston Daily | location=Kingston, New York |date=March 18, 1948 | accessdate=February 17, 2015 }}</ref>{{r|Davis Free Press}} She had set up a trust so that $2,750,000 of her fortune was used for charitable purposes. Her jewelry went to friends and relatives, except for her wedding ring that stayed on her finger, and some of her servants received $10,000. She was buried at the family mausoleum, where she also intended her husband would be buried.{{r|Splendid Suitors}}

A portion of Hill Davis' fortune went to the Poor Sisters of St. Francis ([[Sisters of St. Francis of Perpetual Adoration]]) when they bought the Modern Woodmen Sanatorium and Trianon in [[Colorado Springs, Colorado]] for $1 in 1952.
